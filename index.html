Huntington's Disease

Introduction to Huntington's Disease

Huntington's disease is a rare, inherited condition that affects the brain. Over time, it causes changes in movement, thinking, behaviour, and mood. These changes usually start in adulthood often between the ages of 30 and 50 - and gradually become more noticeable as the disease progresses.

People with Huntington's experience a range of symptoms including uncontrolled movements, changes in thinking, emotional changes and difficulties with everyday tasks. As this condition gradually affects how the brain works, it can have a major impact on daily life.

Huntington's disease is caused by a change to a specific gene, meaning that:

  • A person can have the gene without knowing it.
  • The disease can be passed from a parent to a child.
  • If someone carries the gene, they will develop the condition at some point in life, but we cannot tell when symptoms will start.

For many people, this uncertainty can be one of the biggest challenges. Unless someone chooses to be tested, they simply won't know their genetic status and so will not know if they are predisposed to the disease.

Currently, there is no cure for Huntington's disease, but there are some treatments that can manage symptoms, these include medications for mood or movement. We have the ability to test for the gene that causes the disease either prenatally (during pregnancy) or predictive testing in adults (before symptoms appear). This can give clarity on if the individual will develop the disease, however it also raises some very difficult decisions. Some people will want to know, whilst others would prefer not to.

This leads us to ask, if there is a test for Huntington's disease, a serious health condition with no cure, should we use it? And if we do… who should we test? Adults only or unborn babies as well?

These are the questions at the heart of Huntington's disease and the focus of this project.

Genetics of Huntington's Disease

Huntington's disease is caused by a change in a single gene called the HTT gene. This gene contains a short piece of DNA that repeats the same sequence over and over again - the letters C-A-G (each represents a base of DNA).

You can think of this as a word typed many times in a row: CAGCAGCAGCAGCAG…

The HTT gene normally makes a protein that helps brain cells stay healthy. When the CAG repeat becomes too long, the protein becomes faulty and does not work as well, meaning the brain does not work as well. This faulty protein slowly damages brain cells over many years, leading to symptoms.

Every person has some number of these CAG repeats.

  • Most people have a small number, which is completely harmless.
  • People with Huntington's have a much larger number of repeats than normal.

This is called a repeat expansion.

In simple terms:

  • 40 or more repeats → the person will develop Huntington's at some point in life.
  • 36-39 repeats → symptoms may or may not appear (an uncertain range).
  • 35 or fewer repeats → the person will not develop Huntington's and cannot pass it on.

"Uncertain" clarification - This range behaves unpredictably - some people develop symptoms, while others never do.

The number of repeats a person has can influence how severe the disease is, as influences: the age symptoms begin, and how quickly the condition progresses. In general, the more CAG repeats there are, the earlier the onset of symptoms, and the fewer repeats (but still in the disease range) means later onset. However, the exact timing still varies from person to person.

The number of CAG repeats can sometimes increase when the gene is passed from a parent to a child. This happens more often when the gene comes from the father. If the repeat number grows, the child may have more repeats than the parent - which can mean the disease starts younger. This pattern, where symptoms appear earlier in the next generation, is called anticipation.
